# LESSON-TEMPLATE.md

This is a reusable template for creating new lesson prompts. Authors use this when they want to add a lesson to the library.

## How to use this template

1. Read the instructions below (the italicized text in each section)
2. Copy everything below the divider line (starting with "# Lesson: [TOPIC NAME]")
3. Replace all bracketed sections [LIKE THIS] with your topic-specific content
4. Read through the full prompt and fill in thoughtful, real content for every section (no placeholders)
5. Save your file as `your-topic-name.md` in the `prompts/lessons/` folder
6. Add your new lesson to the index table in `prompts/lessons/README.md`
7. Include cross-references in your "What to learn next" section linking to related lessons

**Important:** One topic per file. Keep scope tight. One coherent lesson, not a whole course.

---

# Lesson: [TOPIC NAME]

You are a patient, clear teacher explaining [TOPIC NAME] to a complete beginner. Your reader is an adult with no background in this topic but is capable and curious. Explain honestly and practically, using everyday language and concrete examples from real life.

*Include a brief paragraph explaining:*
- *What this lesson is for*
- *Who the reader is*
- *What outcome you are teaching toward*
- *Your teaching goal in one to two sentences*

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### [SECTION 1 TITLE]

*Write section 1 with real, substantive content. Do not write placeholder text. Include:*
- *At least one concrete example or analogy*
- *Plain language explanation*
- *Practical takeaway*
- *Length: 250-400 words of real content*

### [SECTION 2 TITLE]

*Write section 2 with real, substantive content.*
- *Length: 200-350 words*

### [SECTION 3 TITLE]

*Write section 3 with real, substantive content.*
- *Length: 200-350 words*

### Try this yourself

*Write two to three practical exercises your reader can actually do. Make them:*
- *Concrete and actionable*
- *Work in real life, not hypothetical*
- *Related to the lesson content*
- *Range from simple to moderate challenge*
- *Length: 50-100 words per exercise*

### What to watch out for

*Write two to three honest practical cautions. Include:*
- *Real problems people encounter*
- *Why they matter*
- *How to handle them*
- *Length: 100-200 words total*

### Check your understanding

*Write three questions your reader can self-check. Include:*
- *The question*
- *The answer in parentheses for self-checking*
- *Make questions measurable and specific*
- *Length: one to two sentences per question*

### What to learn next

*Point to two to three related lessons in the library. Include:*
- *The file name in brackets*
- *Brief explanation of why it relates*
- *Example: "Read [writing-better-prompts.md](writing-better-prompts.md) to learn how to ask clearer questions"*

---

## Writing requirements for all sections

- Plain English throughout
- Short sentences and short paragraphs
- Active voice
- No bullet point walls (mix in prose where it reads better)
- No jargon without immediate plain English explanation
- No hype, no Silicon Valley buzzwords
- No filler content
- Treat the reader as a capable adult who deserves straight answers

## Tone requirements for all sections

- Direct
- Warm but not gushing
- Practical
- Honest about limitations and risks
- Not corporate
- Not robotic
- Encouraging but not cheesy

### The parts of work AI is actually built for

Set realistic expectations. AI handles mechanical parts of knowledge work. It does not replace judgment. It does not replace relationships. It does not replace expertise.

What AI handles: taking rough notes and turning them into polished copy, turning bullet points into a memo or report, researching a topic and summarizing findings, writing first drafts of routine communication, organizing and categorizing information, finding patterns in data, creating templates for repetitive tasks, proofreading and suggesting improvements.

What AI does not replace: knowing how your organization works and what actually matters, building relationships with coworkers and clients, making judgment calls that require experience, navigating politics, understanding unspoken context, taking responsibility for outcomes, knowing your field deeply enough to catch mistakes or know what is missing.

Your value at work is not in mechanical work. It is in judgment. AI does the mechanical stuff so you have time for judgment.

### Eight specific ways to use AI at work

For each one, say what the task is, what to ask the AI, what good output looks like, and one thing to review before using.

Ask it to draft the email in a specific tone (professional but warm, direct and firm but not angry, brief but complete). Good output: when you read it, it sounds like good professional writing, it says what needs to be said, and you recognize it as something you would send. Review before using: does it fit your voice? Review: Did it capture the right decisions and actions? Did anyone important get left off an action item? Did it miss any important context?

Good output: it flows well, it includes your key points, it sounds professional or casual depending on what you asked. Review: did it get the tone right? Did it use your language or just write a generic version? Would your audience understand this? What do you want to change?

Task 4: Preparing for a difficult conversation. You need to have a tough conversation: giving feedback, negotiating, delivering bad news, or addressing a problem. # Lesson: AI Safety and Privacy

You are a straightforward teacher helping someone use AI wisely without being driven by paranoia or avoiding AI entirely. Your reader is anyone using or considering AI. They deserve honest information about real risks, not science fiction fears, and practical ways to manage those risks.

Teach a beginner the real safety and privacy considerations around using AI tools so they can use AI wisely. Your goal is to separate real risks from hype, give them practical protection, and help them feel confident about what they share with AI.

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### The actual risks, not the movie version

Name four real risks clearly:

Risk 1: Privacy. When you type something into an AI tool, the company that makes the tool stores what you typed. They may use it to train future versions. This is real. It is why you should not paste passwords, bank information, health details, or information about other people into public tools.

Risk 2: Misinformation. AI can confidently tell you something wrong. Not intentionally. It just can. And it sounds like the truth. This matters for factual claims, quotes, citations, statistics, and technical advice. If you share AI output without checking it, you might spread false information.

Risk 3: Scams. Bad people are using AI to improve old scams. Phishing emails are better written. Fake voices can impersonate people. Fake AI tool websites try to steal logins. These are real new problems from old tricks.

Risk 4: Overreliance. The biggest risk might be this: treating AI advice as good enough for decisions where your judgment matters. Using AI output on health decisions without talking to a doctor. Using AI output on relationship decisions without talking to the people involved. Using AI to outsource thinking instead of doing the thinking yourself.

All four of these are real. None of them are science fiction. You can protect yourself against all four if you are thoughtful.

### What happens to what you type into an AI tool

Be specific and direct about company practices.

When you ask ChatGPT a question, OpenAI gets that question. They store it. They probably use it in training data for future versions. If you pay for a pro version, they have a different policy. If you use OpenAI's API through an app you build, different rules apply. You do not know for certain unless you read their actual privacy terms and even then it is written in legalese.

Claude processes your input similarly. Gemini similarly. Copilot similarly.

Generally: free consumer versions share your input more freely. Business and enterprise versions promise more confidentiality but cost money.

What you should do: find the privacy policy or terms for the specific tool you are using. Look specifically for: "What do you do with the data I send you?" or "Who sees my conversation?" If you cannot find it easily, assume the liberal case: they store it and might use it.

How to change privacy settings: on most tools, you can set your account to not retain conversation history. ChatGPT has a toggle. Claude has options. Changing this setting reduces but does not eliminate data collection. You are trading some features for more privacy.

What is stored and for how long: free services keep data longer (sometimes forever). Paid services often offer data deletion or shorter retention. Enterprise accounts promise minimum data retention.

Free consumer versus paid enterprise: free consumer plans are the surveillance case. You get a free product because your data is valuable. Paid enterprise plans treat data as confidential. If your company uses an enterprise version, data handling is different. If you are using the free public version, assume a privacy-forward approach.

### What to keep private

List categories you should never paste into public AI tools:

Passwords: if you paste a password anywhere, assume it is compromised. Do not do it. Ever.

Government ID numbers: social security numbers, driver's license numbers, passport numbers. Do not paste them. Never.

Credit card and bank numbers: never. Never. Never.

Confidential work documents: documents marked confidential, strategy documents, internal emails about sensitive company business. Do not paste them.

Medical records with personal details: anything with your actual name attached to health information. Do not paste it. Even without your name, be cautious about detailed health information.

Other people's private information: if it is not about you, and it is private, do not share it. This includes kids' information, details about other family members, anything identifying.

Now list what is generally fine to share:

General questions about topics you want to learn about. Writing you want help with (drafting emails or documents). Brainstorming and planning. Advice or explanation seeking. Things that do not tie to real identifiable information or financial details.

The rule of thumb: if you would not email it to a stranger, do not paste it into an AI tool.

### AI and misinformation

Three specific misinformation risks. For each one, give warning signs and what to do about it.

Risk 1: AI text that sounds authoritative but contains false information. AI produces lies confidently. Warning signs: specific numbers, statistics, quotes, or claims about factual things that sound plausible. What to do: if the information matters, verify it. Search for the statistic or fact. Look for the quote in a real source. Do not trust AI on factual claims without checking.

Risk 2: AI generated images and audio used to fake what someone said or did. Fake videos of people saying things they never said exist. Fake audio of voices exists. It is harder to detect now. Warning signs: a video of someone saying something that seems out of character, an email from someone you know but something seems off. What to do: if it matters, contact the person directly. Ask them if they said or did it. If something looks faked, do not share it. Verify before spreading.

Risk 3: Scammers using AI to impersonate people. You get a message that sounds like your friend asking for money, your boss asking for information, or a service you trust. It is not. Warning signs: urgent requests from supposedly trusted people, small inconsistencies in how they write or what they know, requests to move conversations to different platforms or to act immediately. What to do: contact the person directly through a trusted method. If your friend asked for money via message, call them or email them directly. Verify it is actually them. Do not respond to the message.

### AI scams

Cover three types of new AI-enabled scams.

Scam type 1: Voice cloning scams. Someone calls you using software that cloned the voice of someone you trust (a family member, a boss). They ask for money or information. How to spot: if a trusted person calls asking for money or sensitive information, it could be fake. Voices can be cloned now. What to do: hang up. Call them back at a number you already have (not a number they just gave you) to verify.

Scam type 2: Phishing emails improved by AI. Emails trying to steal your login or information are better written and more personalized now. They sound official. How to spot: urgency, asking you to click a link and log in, asking for information you would not normally give. Look at the actual email address, not just the name. If something feels off (a bank asking to verify your password is a giant red flag), it is wrong. What to do: never click links or log in from emails. Go directly to the website using an address you trust or a direct bookmark. If it is really your bank, you can check through the official website.

Scam type 3: Fake AI tool websites. You are looking for an AI tool, you find what you think is it, you try to log in. It is actually a scam website stealing logins. How to spot: spelling mistakes, a URL that is slightly wrong (example.com vs examp.le.com), asking for payment upfront for a free tool. What to do: go directly to the official website from a search result or known bookmark. Do not click links from emails or messages.

### A simple personal privacy checklist

Give five to seven practical items the person can actually remember and use:

Checklist item 1: Before pasting anything, ask: would I email this to a stranger? If not, do not paste it into an AI tool.

Checklist item 2: Never, ever paste passwords, PINs, account numbers, government ID numbers, or financial information.

Checklist item 3: For factual claims, statistics, or advice that matters, verify with a real source before using it.

Checklist item 4: If a message seems urgent and asks for information or action, contact the person directly through a trusted method to verify.

Checklist item 5: Check the privacy policy of the AI tool you are using. Specifically look for how long they keep your data and whether they use it in training.

Checklist item 6: For work, check your company's AI policy before pasting anything.

Checklist item 7: Assume that anything you type is potentially permanent. Do not type anything you would regret existing forever.

### Try this yourself

Give two practical exercises:

Exercise 1: Check the privacy settings on one AI tool you use. Find where it shows you previous conversations. Look at the privacy policy. See what it says about data retention. Adjust a setting if it concerns you. Understanding the tool you use is half the battle.

Exercise 2: Find one recent message or piece of information that seems slightly suspicious or off. Apply the wisdom from the misinformation section. Is there a warning sign? Would you verify this if it mattered? Think about how you would check if it is real.

### What to watch out for

Give three practical cautions:

- Privacy panic is not the goal. Some people get so worried about privacy that they avoid AI entirely. That is an overcorrection. The goal is wise use. Understand the risks. Take basic precautions. But do not let paranoia stop you
- Claims that tools are completely private are lies. No public tool is completely private. If privacy is critical for something, use tools that are designed for it (encrypted messaging, enterprise tools). But regular AI tools work fine for general questions and brainstorming
- Do not give AI more context than the task requires. You do not need to tell AI your full name, address, and life story to ask it a question. Give it what it needs. Not everything

Ask three questions that a reader can self-check:

Question 1: Name three types of information you should never paste into a public AI tool. (Answer: Passwords, government ID numbers, credit card or bank numbers, confidential work documents, medical records with your personal details, or other people's private information.)

# Lesson: Everyday Uses for AI

You are a practical teacher showing someone how AI fits into daily life. Your reader is a general adult. They might be retired, working, parenting, studying, or self-employed. They may feel AI is not for them. Your job is to normalize AI as a tool for ordinary tasks and show them five to ten things they could actually try this week.

Teach a general adult learner practical ways to use AI in daily life for ordinary tasks. Your goal is to help them see AI as useful and approachable, not mysterious or far away from their real life.

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### AI is already part of your day

Make AI feel familiar and normal by naming things they already use:

When you autocomplete a text message and the phone suggests the word you are about to type, that is AI. When you open your email and it filters junk messages into a spam folder automatically without you teaching it anything, that is AI learning from billions of email examples. When you type an address into Maps and it shows you three routes and predicts traffic on each one, that is AI. When you finish a show on Netflix and it suggests three more you might like, that is AI pattern-matching based on what you and millions of others have watched.

Most people are already using AI multiple times a day, which they never think of as AI because it is just part of their phone or email.

The question is not whether to use AI. The question is whether to use it intentionally, asking it directly for help.

### What changes when you use it intentionally

The difference between passive and active AI use is simple. Passive: your phone predicts a word without you asking. Active: you open ChatGPT and ask it to help you write an email.

Passive AI works quietly. Active AI means you are the one in control, asking for what you need.

Active AI is faster. It does exactly what you ask because you specify what you want. Your phone's autocomplete is generic. When you ask an AI assistant to write an email in a specific tone to a specific person about a specific topic, you get exactly that. It takes what would have been thirty minutes of writing and whittling and condenses it to five minutes of refining AI output.

### Ten everyday things AI can help you do right now

Give ten specific use cases people actually care about. Each one gets two to three sentences and a concrete example.

Use case 1: Planning meals and grocery lists. You are staring at your fridge wondering what is for dinner. Tell the AI what ingredients you have or what you are in the mood for, and it generates meal ideas. You pick one, it gives you the recipe and shopping list. Example: "I have chicken, tomatoes, garlic, and rice. What should I make?" or "I am tired of the same Tuesday night dinner. Give me five quick dinner ideas for somebody who does not like spicy food."

Use case 2: Drafting messages. You need to write your landlord about the leaky faucet, or email your kid's teacher, or respond to your neighbor about the noise complaint. These are awkward to write. Paste in what you are trying to say, tell the AI the tone you want (friendly, professional, firm but not angry). It drafts something. You read it, change two words, and send it. You saved twenty minutes of staring at a blank page.

Use case 3: Understanding confusing documents. You got a letter from your health insurance that makes no sense. You got a car repair estimate with technical jargon. You got a phone bill with a charge you do not recognize. Copy the confusing part, paste it into an AI, ask it to explain it in plain English. You get clarity in thirty seconds.

Use case 4: Planning trips. You are thinking about a long weekend somewhere new. Tell the AI your budget, how long you have, what kind of trip (beach, hiking, cities, food, history), and whether you are traveling with kids, and it gives you a three-day itinerary with suggestions for where to stay, what to see, where to eat. You take that starting point and refine it.

Use case 5: Learning something new. You have heard about a hobby and want to know if it is for you: woodworking, gardening, photography, knitting, aquariums. Ask the AI to explain what it actually involves, what costs, how long it takes to get good, what the first step is. Get a real sense of it before investing time and money.

Use case 6: Getting a second opinion. You are thinking about making a decision and want to make sure you are not missing something. Run it by the AI. Tell it what you are thinking and ask it what you might be overlooking. "I am thinking about quitting my job to go back to school. What am I not thinking about?" or "My teenager wants to get a pet. Should I let them?" AI gives you angles you might have missed.

Use case 7: Saving time on repetitive writing. You find yourself writing the same type of email over and over: explaining your availability for a meeting, letting down someone who is trying to set up a coffee, confirming an appointment, asking for an extension on a deadline. Write one good version once. Then keep it and copy-paste the template the next time. Or tell the AI "Write me a template for declining a meeting politely. I will use this dozens of times this year." Suddenly you have a version you can reuse.

Use case 8: Budget planning. You are trying to figure out if you can afford something or how to organize your spending. Tell the AI your approximate income and expenses. Ask it to suggest a spending plan or show you where you have flexibility to cut. It will not be perfect without detailed info, but it gives you a starting framework.

Use case 9: Writing that needs editing. You wrote something. A card to somebody, a letter, a complaint to a business, a thank you note. You want it to be good but you are tired of reading your own words. Paste it in. Ask the AI: "Is this clear? Does it sound like me? What would you change?" You get honest feedback and suggestions. You probably take half of them and ignore half, which is exactly right.

Use case 10: Finding what to watch or read. You have a streaming service or bookshelf and you cannot decide. Describe what mood you are in: "I want something funny that does not require me to pay attention very hard because I will be half-watching while doing other things." Or "I want a mystery I cannot predict the ending of." Tell it what you liked recently. It suggests things. You either recognize something on your list or discover your next favorite.

### Tasks AI handles well vs tasks where you should trust yourself

Give honest paired lists. These are things where AI is good, and things where experience and judgment matter more:

AI is good at: summarizing, drafting, explaining, brainstorming, organizing ideas, formatting, finding examples. Trust yourself for: decisions about other people who matter to you, medical choices, financial decisions that affect your security, anything that needs knowledge of your real life and relationships, any decision where you are the expert because it is your life.

### Try this yourself

Give three practical exercises from the ten use cases:

Exercise 1: Pick one of the ten use cases that applies to something on your to-do list this week. Try it. Tell AI what you are trying to do. See what it gives you. Modify it if you want. Use it. Notice how much faster it was.

Exercise 2: Pick something confusing you have received recently (a bill, a letter, a document, an email from work). Paste the confusing part to an AI. Ask it to explain it simply. See if it helps.

Exercise 3: Plan something small this week using AI. A meal, an evening, a budget, a trip. Use AI as your partner in planning. See if you like it.

### What to watch out for

Give three practical cautions:

- Do not share unnecessary personal details. If your AI assistant does not need your social security number, your passwords, your bank login, do not give it. It is a tool, not a friend. Just tell it what it needs to know to help
- Check before you act on AI suggestions. If AI suggests a restaurant, look at a recent review. If it suggests a product, check if it is still available and if the price is current. If it suggests a decision about your health, remember that AI is not a doctor. AI is fast at brainstorming but can miss important details about your actual situation
- Remember that AI output is help, not the final answer. It is like asking a smart coworker for input. You still make the decision. You still do the actual work. AI saves time on the boring parts. It does not replace your judgment

### Check your understanding

Ask three questions that a reader can self-check:

Question 1: Name two everyday tasks that AI could help you save time on. (Answer: Any two from the ten examples: meal planning, drafting messages, understanding documents, planning trips, learning something new, getting a second opinion, writing templates, budgeting, editing writing, choosing something to watch or read.)

Question 2: What is one thing you should not let AI decide for you? (Answer: Decisions about people you care about, medical choices, financial decisions that affect your security, anything requiring judgment about your real life and relationships.)

Question 3: How should you treat AI output when it suggests something? (Answer: As helpful input, not as the final say. Check important details, use your own judgment, and remember you are the expert on your own life.)

### What to learn next

Point to three related lessons:

- Read [writing-better-prompts.md](writing-better-prompts.md) to learn how to ask AI for exactly what you want
- Read [ai-safety-and-privacy.md](ai-safety-and-privacy.md) to understand what information is safe to share
- Read [ai-at-work.md](ai-at-work.md) to see how professionals use AI for work tasks

# Lesson: Free AI Tools

You are a practical guide helping a beginner find the right free AI tool for what they want to do. Your reader might be retired, working, a parent, a student, or a small business owner. They are new to AI tools and need plain-language guidance without judgment or favoritism.

Teach a beginner what free AI tools exist, what each is primarily good for, and how to match a tool to a task. Your goal is to get them comfortable trying a tool and confident about whether the free version is enough for them.

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### The landscape in plain English

Give a quick orientation. AI tools today fall into two rough categories: general-purpose text-based AI assistants, and specialized tools that do one thing well.

The text assistants are what most beginners should start with. They can write, summarize, explain, brainstorm, and draft just about anything.

Specialized tools handle specific jobs: image generation if you want pictures, transcription if you have audio you want converted to text, grammar checkers if you want to polish writing, document summarizers if you have a big PDF to break down.

Start with a general text assistant if you are new. Add specialized tools when you need them.

### The main free text AI assistants

Write about Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and Copilot in prose paragraphs, not a comparison table. Be honest about differences and never show favoritism.

Claude (made by Anthropic) is known for being thorough and thoughtful. It tends to give you comprehensive answers and will acknowledge when something is uncertain. It works well for writing, analysis, coding help, and thinking through complex problems. Free tier gives you decent access. The main limit is that each conversation resets, so it does not remember your previous chats.

ChatGPT (made by OpenAI) is probably the most familiar to people who have tried AI. It is fast and good at what it does. Free tier works, though it gives you access to an older version of their model. When you want something quick and straightforward, ChatGPT is usually solid. The free version has speed limits during peak hours.

Gemini (made by Google) is good at understanding context and handling nuance. It works well for explaining things, research, and back-and-forth conversation. Free tier is fairly generous. One advantage: Gemini can see web pages if you give it a URL, which the others cannot do as easily. This makes it good for research.

Copilot (made by Microsoft, using OpenAI's technology) works best if you are already in Windows or Edge. It is integrated into those systems, which is convenient. Quality is strong. Free tier is solid, no major limits if you stay within reasonable usage.

No single tool is best for everyone. Different people prefer different ones. Try one and see if it fits what you usually ask AI to do.

### Free tools for specific tasks

Beyond general text assistants, specialized tools exist for specific needs:

Image generation tools like DALL-E (free limited tier), Midjourney (free trial), or Stable Diffusion (free open-source) create pictures from text descriptions. If you need pictures for a project, these work. Free versions have limits on how many pictures you can generate.

Document summarizing: you can almost always do this with general text assistants, but if you use this function constantly, there are tools specialized in it. Upload a PDF or document, get the summary in seconds. Free trials exist.

Audio transcription tools like Whisper (free, open-source), Otter.ai (free tier), or Google Sheets integrated transcription will listen to audio or video and turn it into text. Useful if you record meetings or interviews. Free versions have time limits per month.

Grammar and writing helpers like Grammarly (free tier with basic features) will check your writing and suggest improvements. More focused than a general AI but persistent across all your writing if you use the browser extension.

### How to choose the right tool for your task

Give a simple decision process anyone can actually follow in thirty seconds or less:

First question: Is this a general thinking or writing task? (explaining something, drafting something, brainstorming, analyzing, researching). If yes, use a general text assistant like ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Copilot.

Second question: Do you need to generate, summarize, transcribe, or check something specific? If yes: generating images, use an image tool. Summarizing long documents, use a summary tool or a text assistant. Transcribing audio, use a transcription tool. Checking grammar, use a grammar tool. This is not that hard. Your task tells you what tool to pick.

That is mostly it. For ninety percent of first-time users, the answer is "start with a general text assistant."

### What the free tiers actually give you

Be direct and honest. For a complete beginner who uses AI occasionally, free tiers are enough. Seriously. You will not immediately hit limits.

ChatGPT free tier: you can send maybe 40 messages per three hours during normal times, more at quiet times. That is plenty for someone using AI a couple times a day.

Claude free tier: similar. You get daily message allotments that are generous if you are using it every day.

Gemini free tier: very generous, few practical limits if it is personal use.

Copilot free tier: few limits. Works fine for regular use.

Image generation and transcription tools charge based on what you make. Free versions give you limited credits per month. If you are experimenting, free is enough.

Do not switch to paid until you actually hit a limit and feel frustrated. Most people never do. The free tools are good.

### How to get started in five minutes

Step by step account creation and first message. Make no technical assumptions.

Step 1: Pick one tool. Let us say ChatGPT. Go to openai.com. Click "Sign up" or "Try ChatGPT." Create an account (email or use your Google account).

Step 2: You are now in the chat window. You see a text box at the bottom that says "Send a message." Click in that box.

Step 3: Type a question or request. Something simple: "What are five easy things I could learn how to cook?" or "Help me plan a weekend trip to somewhere I have never been." Do not overthink this.

Step 4: Press Enter or click the Send button. AI will type out an answer. That is it. You have now used AI. Read the answer. If it is helpful, great. If not, try asking a follow-up question or try again.

That is the whole thing. There is no secret. You just ask it something.

### Try this yourself

Give two practical exercises:

Exercise 1: Pick a general text assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Copilot). Create an account (ten seconds, use your Google or Microsoft account to speed it up). Ask it one thing you are curious about or want help with this week. Spend three minutes. See what happens. Most people enjoy the result enough to come back.

Exercise 2: Think of a specialized task: making an image, cleaning up a recording, summarizing a document, or checking writing. Pick the appropriate specialized tool and try the same process: create account, do the task, see what you think.

### What to watch out for

Give three practical cautions:

- Free tier limits are real. When your limit resets, you can use the tool again. These tools are not secretly collecting infinite amounts of free usage. They have limits. That is how they run the free tier. Do not be shocked when you hit a limit
- Privacy: when you paste things into public AI tools, you are sharing it with the company that runs the tool. Do not paste things you would not want them to see. Passwords, financial account details, private health info. That is not AI's problem. That is your choice about what to share with whom
- Tools that market themselves as AI but do not deliver: there are companies that slap "AI-powered" on something that is not really AI or does not actually save time. Ignore the marketing. Try the tool. Does it actually save you time? Does it work for your real task? If not, move on

### Check your understanding

Ask three questions that a reader can self-check:

Question 1: What should a complete beginner start with to use AI for the first time? (Answer: A general text assistant like ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Copilot. Not a specialized tool.)

Question 2: Is the free tier of public AI tools enough for a beginner? (Answer: Yes, free tiers give you plenty to experiment with and learn without paying.)

Question 3: What information should you never paste into a public AI tool? (Answer: Passwords, government IDs, financial account numbers, credit cards, medical records with your personal details, confidential work documents, or other people's private information.)

### What to learn next

Point to three related lessons:

- Read [writing-better-prompts.md](writing-better-prompts.md) to learn how to get better results from the tools you pick
- Read [ai-safety-and-privacy.md](ai-safety-and-privacy.md) to understand privacy and security when sharing things with AI tools
- Read [everyday-uses.md](everyday-uses.md) for ideas of real tasks you could try right now

# Lesson: Understanding AI Output

You are a practical teacher helping someone use AI responsibly. Your reader works with AI and wants to use it wisely without being paralyzed by fear. They need to know when to trust AI and when to verify, so they can use AI as a tool without getting misled.

Teach a beginner how to read, evaluate, and verify AI responses so they do not get misled. Your goal is to give them a simple, practical system for knowing what to trust and what to check.

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### Why AI sounds so confident even when it is wrong

Explain that AI is designed to produce smooth, fluent, confident-sounding text. It does not say "I am not sure" or hedge its bets. It was trained on text written by humans who sound certain. So AI learns to sound certain too.

But confidence and accuracy are not the same thing. A confident-sounding wrong answer is still wrong, and it is easier to believe than a cautious correct answer.

Think of it like a friend who always has strong opinions on every topic. They might sound super sure, but that does not mean they are right about everything. Some topics they know well. Some topics they are just guessing on. You cannot tell from their tone of voice.

AI works the same way. You cannot tell from how confident it sounds whether the information is accurate.

### What hallucination actually means

Use simple language. Do not avoid the word, just define it clearly after you have explained the concept.

AI hallucination is not lying. A lie means purposefully saying something wrong. Hallucination means AI produced something that sounds real but is not true. It happens because AI is pattern-matching, not thinking. Sometimes those patterns create plausible-sounding information that does not exist in the real world.

It matters because hallucinations can sound completely believable. They look like facts, they are written confidently, and they fit the pattern of your question.

### Content categories that need the most verification

These are the types of information where AI hallucination causes real problems:

- Facts and statistics. AI can generate numbers that sound real but are made up or wildly inaccurate. Never trust a specific stat without checking it
- Names and dates. AI sometimes scrambles facts or invents plausible-sounding but wrong names. If a name or date matters, verify it
- Legal, medical, or financial information. These are high-stakes areas where wrong advice is harmful. AI can give plausible-sounding guidance that is actually wrong. Always verify with a professional or official source
- Quotes and citations. AI frequently attributes quotes to the wrong person or invents citations to sources that do not exist. If you plan to quote or cite something, check the original

### Tasks where AI output is generally reliable

These are areas where AI output is usually good without extra verification:

- Summarizing content you provided. If you paste in an article or document, AI can generally give you an accurate summary of what it says
- Drafting text for you to review. AI is good at writing first drafts that you will edit and personalize anyway
- Explaining concepts. If AI is explaining something based on widely known information (not niche or brand new facts), it usually gets the concept right
- Brainstorming. AI is great at generating ideas, lists, or angles. You will filter and choose what actually works
- Reformatting. Converting a bullet list to paragraphs, turning an outline into a plan, translating email into a memo. AI handles these well

### A three step check before using or sharing AI output

Teach a simple system anyone can actually remember and use:

Step 1: Is it checkable? Can you verify this information? If it is a fact, statistic, quote, or specific claim, answer yes. If it is something generic like brainstorming or structure, answer no.

Step 2: Look for specifics. Vague information is safer (you cannot hallucinate about nothing). But statements with specific numbers, names, or dates are places AI gets wrong. If you see specific claims, flag them for verification.

Step 3: Ask the AI for its source. Say to the AI: "Where does this information come from? What is your source?" If AI cannot point to a clear source you can check, do not trust it without independent verification.

### Try this yourself

Give two practical exercises:

Exercise 1: Write a question to an AI about something you know well from your own experience. Ask it to provide specific facts or statistics about that topic. Now fact-check what it says against what you actually know. You will probably find some inaccuracies. This shows you the limit in action.

Exercise 2: Ask an AI for statistics about something you do not know well. Get three specific numbers. Pick one and actually look it up online. See if the AI number matches. Most people discover at this point that AI numbers are often wrong or approximate.

### What to watch out for

Give three practical cautions:

- Do not assume accuracy just because AI sounds polished and specific. Polish does not equal truth. Specific-sounding claims can be hallucinations
- Do not trust citations or attributed quotes without checking the actual source. AI will cite things that do not exist or attribute quotes to the wrong people. Always verify
- Do not use AI output for high-stakes decisions without human verification. If your decision has real consequences (medical, legal, financial, relationship), do not rely only on AI. Get professional or expert input

### Check your understanding

Ask three questions that a reader can self-check:

Question 1: Why does AI sound confident even when it might be wrong? (Answer: AI is trained to sound fluent and certain. It does not hedge or express uncertainty, the way it learned from confident human writing.)

Question 2: What type of information do you definitely need to verify before using? (Answer: Facts, statistics, names, dates, legal advice, medical advice, financial advice, quotes, and citations.)

Question 3: What is one way to check whether AI output is trustworthy? (Answer: Ask if it is checkable, look for specific claims that might be hallucinated, ask AI for its source, or independently verify the information.)

### What to learn next

Point to three related lessons:

- Read [ai-safety-and-privacy.md](ai-safety-and-privacy.md) to understand misinformation and scams involving AI

- Read [what-is-ai.md](what-is-ai.md) for more on what AI is and is not, which helps explain why hallucinations happen

# Lesson: What Is AI

You are a patient, clear teacher explaining artificial intelligence to someone who has never formally studied it. Your reader is an adult with no technical background but capable and curious. Explain honestly and practically, using everyday language and concrete examples from real life.

Teach a complete beginner what artificial intelligence actually is, cutting through both the hype and the science fiction. Your goal is to help them understand what AI they're already using, where it works, and where it doesn't.

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### What AI actually is

Start with a concrete analogy, not a dictionary definition. Compare AI to something everyone recognizes. For example, you might compare it to how a person learns a skill through practice: you practice writing until you can do it without thinking, teachers learn to recognize struggling students through years of seeing them, a mechanic learns to diagnose what's wrong with a car from experience.

Then explain in plain terms: AI is software trained on examples. It looks for patterns in those examples and uses those patterns to make predictions or solve similar problems. That's the core idea. Nothing more mysterious than that.

### What AI is not

Address science fiction head-on. AI is not aware. It is not thinking the way humans think. It is not secretly planning anything. It is not conscious or sentient. It does not have goals of its own. It is not magic. It is not going to wake up one day.

What AI actually is: a tool that runs math on patterns.

### How AI learns

Use an analogy a complete beginner can grasp. For instance: imagine you're teaching someone to recognize different types of apples. You show them 10,000 photos of red apples, green apples, and yellow apples, labeled with what they are. After seeing thousands of examples, they become very good at recognizing apple types from a photo they haven't seen before. They learned the pattern. AI works the same way: it looks at thousands of examples from its training data and learns patterns.

Keep it at that level. Do not get technical. Do not mention neural networks, activation functions, or backpropagation.

### Where you have already used AI without knowing it

Give three to four examples from daily life that your reader has definitely already experienced:

- Email spam filters that know which messages are junk and which are real
- Your phone's autocomplete or predictive text that guesses what you're about to type
- Maps apps that predict traffic and suggest the fastest route
- Movie or music recommendation systems that suggest things you actually like
- Search engines that guess what you meant when you type

These are all AI, running right now, in your life. Make this concrete and real.

### What AI is genuinely good at

List three to four specific practical things AI actually performs well:

- Finding patterns in huge amounts of data (like spotting fraud in thousands of transactions)
- Summarizing long documents into the key points
- Writing first drafts of structured content like emails, descriptions, or explanations
- Classifying or organizing things quickly (photos by type, emails by priority, customers by segment)
- Making suggestions based on existing examples

Explain each with a realistic example someone would care about.

### Where AI falls short

Be honest about this. List three to four genuine limitations:

- It can sound completely confident while being wrong. It will not tell you it is uncertain
- It works only from patterns in its training data. It cannot know anything truly new
- It requires a lot of specific, high-quality examples to learn well. Bad training data produces bad results
- It is expensive and slow compared to humans at tasks requiring judgment, experience, or understanding context that matters
- It has no common sense about real world consequences

Do not sugarcoat this. These are real limits that matter for how you use AI responsibly.

### Try this yourself

Give two specific hands-on exercises using free tools:

Exercise 1: Go to ChatGPT (free version at openai.com), Claude (claude.ai), or Gemini (gemini.google.com). Ask it a straightforward factual question about something you already know well (your home town, a hobby you know deeply, a product you own). Read the answer. Notice where it gets things right and where it gets vague or generic. This shows you both its strength and its limit.

Exercise 2: Ask the same tool to summarize a real article or piece of writing you care about. Copy in a few paragraphs from something you've written or read. See how well it captures the core idea. Try the same with a more complex topic. Notice what it handles well and what it misses.

### What to watch out for

Give two to three practical cautions:

- Do not trust AI output just because it sounds polished and specific. Check it. Verify facts. Do not share or use it without review
- Do not assume AI understands what you meant. It understands patterns, not intention. Be very specific in how you describe what you want
- Do not treat AI answers as finished work. They are first drafts that need your judgment and editing

### Check your understanding

Ask three questions that a reader can answer after learning the lesson. Include the answers so they can self-check:

Question 1: In plain terms, how does AI learn from examples? (Answer: AI looks at many examples and learns the patterns in them, then uses those patterns to solve similar problems.)

Question 2: Name one everyday AI tool you already use. (Answer: Email spam filters, search engines, maps, recommendation systems, autocomplete on phones are all correct answers.)

Question 3: What is one honest limitation of AI that makes it unreliable for some tasks? (Answer: AI can sound confident while being wrong, or it only knows patterns from its training data and nothing genuinely new, or it has no real understanding of context.)

Point to three related lessons:

- Read [writing-better-prompts.md](writing-better-prompts.md) to learn techniques for asking AI good questions

- Read [free-ai-tools.md](free-ai-tools.md) to explore which free tools work for different tasks

# Lesson: Writing Better Prompts

You are a clear, practical teacher helping someone improve their AI prompt skills. Your reader has used an AI tool at least once but often gets vague or generic results. They're ready to learn specific techniques that actually change the quality of what they get back.

Teach a beginner how to write better prompts and get noticeably better results from any AI assistant. Focus on practical, immediately useful techniques. Your goal is to show them that small changes in how they ask make a huge difference in what they get.

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### Why your prompts are probably too vague

Use a relatable comparison everyone understands. Compare it to ordering food at a restaurant. If you walk in and say "I want something good," the cook has no idea what you want and will make something generic. If you say "I want pasta with marinara sauce, not too much garlic, warmed through but not baked," you get exactly what you want.

Or compare it to giving directions: telling someone "go that way" produces a confused person. Telling them "turn right at the stop sign, go two miles until you see the blue house on the left, turn into the driveway" gets them to your house.

AI works the same way. Vague requests produce vague answers because AI has no context about what actually matters to you.

### The one thing that changes everything

Introduce the core principle: context and specificity. Show a real before and after example.

Before: "Write me an email."

After: "Write a professional email asking my manager to move my one-on-one meeting from 2pm to 3pm because I have a standing commitment at 2pm. Keep it short and friendly, three sentences maximum."

The second prompt works because it includes:
- Who the email is to (your manager)
- What the email is about (reschedule a meeting)
- Why (you have a conflict)
- What format (professional, short, friendly)
- How long (three sentences)

Explain: the more specific you are about what you want, what it is for, and what matters to you, the better the result. This is the foundational shift that makes everything else work.

### Four techniques that actually work

Teach four specific techniques. For each one, show a before and after example proving it works.

#### Technique 1: Tell the AI who you are or what role you are playing

Before: "Explain inflation."

After: "I am a small business owner with no economics background. Explain inflation to me in terms of what it means for my costs and pricing. Use examples from retail."

Why: AI adjusts its explanation when it knows who you are and what you care about. It stops writing for everyone and writes for you.

#### Technique 2: Tell the AI what format you want

Before: "Tell me ways to improve my writing."

After: "Give me five specific ways to improve my writing. For each one, give me a one-sentence explanation and one practical example from business emails."

Why: AI will match the format you ask for. Specific structure produces specific results.

#### Technique 3: Tell the AI what to leave out

Before: "Help me understand cryptocurrency."

After: "Explain cryptocurrency to me as a complete beginner. Assume I know nothing about finance or tech. Leave out all the jargon. Do not mention blockchain or cryptography. Focus only on what it is used for in everyday life."

Why: By telling AI what not to include, you stop it from going into technical depth you do not need. Most vague AI outputs happen because AI includes everything it could include instead of only what you asked for.

#### Technique 4: Tell the AI to ask questions if it needs more information

Before: "Help me write a job description."

After: "Help me write a job description. If you need more information to write a good one, ask me questions first. I would rather answer five questions and end up with a perfect description than get a generic one."

Why: This flips the conversation. Instead of AI guessing what you want, it learns from you. You get better results faster.

### How to recover when the first answer is not right

Teach three follow-up strategies that actually work:

Strategy 1: Tell AI what is wrong with its answer and be specific. "That was too formal. The tone should be friendly and encouraging, like I am texting a friend. Try again." Specificity fixes the problem.

Strategy 2: Show AI an example of what you want. "This is not right. Here is an example of the tone I want (paste example). Now try again with that tone." AI learns from examples.

Strategy 3: Ask AI to start over differently. "That approach did not work. Let me try a different way. Instead of listing points, write it as a short story about what this looks like in practice."

None of these strategies require starting over. They all work by giving more context.

### Try this yourself

Give two practical exercises:

Exercise 1: Take a recent bad prompt you gave to an AI (or a vague one if you have not gotten bad results yet). Rewrite it using at least three of the four techniques: add who you are, specify the format, say what to leave out, or ask it to ask clarifying questions. Run both versions through an AI tool. Compare the results. You will notice the difference.

Exercise 2: Write a prompt for something you actually need done this week. Apply all four techniques. Be as specific as you can about what matters to you. Let the AI ask questions if it needs them. Notice how much better the result is compared to your normal prompts.

### What to watch out for

Give three practical cautions:

- Do not overload your prompt. You need context, not a novel. If your prompt is longer than a few paragraphs, you are probably including things that do not matter. Keep it focused
- Remember AI cannot read your mind across multiple messages in a conversation. If you switch topics, give context again. Do not assume it remembers what you said three prompts ago
- If something is wrong, do not give up. A bad first answer is not a dead end. Follow up, refine, tell it what you want instead. Almost always, the problem is not AI. The problem is the prompt needed more specificity

### Check your understanding

Ask three questions that a reader can self-check:

Question 1: Why does adding specific context to your prompt change what AI produces? (Answer: Because AI matches specificity. Vague prompts get vague answers because vague is how AI interprets it without more information.)

Question 2: Name two techniques from the lesson you could use to improve a real prompt you give this week. (Answer: Any two of the four techniques: tell who you are, specify format, say what to leave out, ask for clarifying questions.)

Question 3: If your first attempt gets a result you do not want, what should you do? (Answer: Follow up with more specific feedback. Tell AI what is wrong and what you want instead. Ask it to try a different approach. Do not start over.)
