# Lesson: Using AI in Small Business

You are a practical guide for small business owners and self-employed people new to AI. Your reader is time-pressed, skeptical of complexity and expensive tools, and thinking about whether AI is actually worth their time. They do not have a tech background. Your job is to show them concrete time and money savings, realistic expectations, and warnings about what not to do.

Teach a small business owner specific ways to use AI to save time, reduce costs, and get more done without hiring more people. Your goal is to help them see where AI actually fits into their business operations and give them confidence to try it without overselling what AI can do.

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### The honest case for AI in a small business

Be direct. No overselling.

Small businesses cannot afford someone for every task. You wear many hats. AI cannot wear hats. But AI can do certain repetitive parts of your hat-wearing faster, which frees your time for the things only you can do.

AI saves hours on tasks like writing marketing copy repeatedly, summarizing customer feedback, responding to routine inquiries, organizing data, creating templates you use over and over. It does not replace real customer service, your judgment about your business, or the expertise that makes your business valuable.

If you are drowning in junk that takes time but does not matter, AI can help you stop drowning. You put that time back toward growing the business.

### Ten things AI can do for your business right now

Give ten use cases with realistic small business scenarios for each.

Use case 1: Writing and editing marketing copy. You need product descriptions, emails to your customer list, social media posts, or website copy. Instead of staring at a blank page, tell the AI what you are selling, who your customer is, what matters about it, what you want the copy to do. It drafts something. You edit it to sound like you and add details only you know. You go from two hours of writing to thirty minutes of tweaking. Example: AI writes the product descriptions for your online store. You review them, add a detail about quality or origin only you know, and use them.

Use case 2: Customer communication. You get similar questions or requests repeatedly. Someone wants to know shipping times. Someone asks about returns. Someone wants a quote. Build templates. Then either use them directly or ask AI to adapt your template for a specific customer. Example: instead of writing an answer to "Do you ship internationally?" twenty times, you have one good answer you send. AI helps you maintain and personalize it quickly.

Use case 3: Social media content ideas. You do not know what to post this week. Tell the AI what you sell, what matters about it, your audience, and the platform. Ask for ten post ideas. You pick two or three, flesh them out, and schedule them. Example: you tell AI you run a bakery, love featuring behind-the-scenes stories, and post on Instagram. AI generates ideas. You pick "behind the scenes of prep for Saturday's wedding cake" and "five reasons bakes taste better warm."

Use case 4: Creating systems and checklists. You do certain tasks repeatedly and you are tired of reinventing the wheel. Tell AI what you need to do repeatedly and why it matters. Ask it to build a checklist, system, or process document you can use or hand off to someone else. Example: you have to do an inventory count once a month. AI builds your inventory checklist template. You use the same one every month. If you hire someone, they have a system to follow.

Use case 5: Research and competitive analysis. You want to understand your market, see what competitors are doing, understand a trend, or research whether your pricing is reasonable. Ask the AI to brief you on the landscape. Example: you think about raising your prices. Ask AI about pricing strategies for your type of business, or ask it to explain what you should think about when raising prices.

Use case 6: Email and sales follow-up. You talk to prospects and then lose track of them. Build templates for follow-up. Ask the AI to draft a friendly follow-up to send a week later if they have not responded. Example: someone inquires about your service but does not sign up. A week later, AI helps you draft "just checking in to see if you had questions about our services, happy to chat anytime" and you send it.

Use case 7: Financial and business administration. You need to create an invoice template, a contract or agreement, an employee handbook summary, expense tracking categories, a business plan outline. AI can build first drafts of all of these. Example: you need an invoice template. Tell AI what information you need, what your business looks like. It builds you a template you customize and then reuse.

Use case 8: Content organization. You have a pile of customer feedback, reviews, sales data, or information. Ask the AI to organize it, find themes, or summarize key insights. Example: you have a folder of emails with customer feature requests. Tell the AI your business and paste in a bunch of requests. Ask it to group them by theme. You see immediately what customers actually want.

Use case 9: Hiring and recruiting. You need to write a job posting. You got a bunch of applications and need to pick out the interesting ones. You want to prepare interview questions. AI can help with all of this. Example: tell the AI what the job is, what your company is like, what you are looking for. It writes you a job posting. You edit it and use it.

Use case 10: Brainstorming and planning. You are stuck on something or you are planning something new. Tell the AI what you are stuck on and ask for angles. Example: your sales are flat and you do not know why. Tell that to an AI with context about your business. Ask for possibilities you might be missing. You get a brain dump of ideas to explore.

### What AI saves you and what it costs

Be honest about time savings and realistic about costs.

Free AI tools can do all ten of those things. Free. You invest time learning to use them and experimenting. You save maybe five to ten hours per month on repetitive tasks depending on how much repetitive stuff you do. That is real time. That is time you get back.

Paid versions of AI tools start at around ten dollars a month and go up from there. Whether it makes sense depends on how much you use it and what kind of business you run. Service businesses that write a lot of custom proposals might get more value than retail businesses. Experiment with free first. Move to paid only when free limits are actually in your way.

What AI cannot replace: the work that is actually your business. If you are a consultant, AI helps you draft and write, but you are still meeting clients and doing the work. If you are a maker (baker, contractor, artist), AI helps you talk about your work, but you are still making it. If you are a shop, AI helps you describe things and communicate with customers, but you are still buying, stocking, and selling actual things.

### Customer-facing content: what to always review before publishing

This is the most important part. What you put in front of customers matters.

Generic-sounding copy is a risk. If your AI-drafted description sounds like it could be for anyone's business, you have not personalized it enough. Fix this. Add specific details about quality, origins, your personal philosophy, what makes it different from the generic thing.

Accuracy of product claims is critical. If the AI generates a claim about what your product does or how it works, you verify that. You are the expert on your product. AI is not. Do not publish a claim you know is not right just because AI wrote it.

Legal language needs professional review. Do not let AI write your terms and conditions, privacy policy, or contracts without having a lawyer look at it. AI sounds official but is not legal advice.

Personal email responses can be from the template but should not be exactly copy-pasted. Your customer deserves to feel like you are responding to them, not sending a form letter. Use AI templates as starting points. Then personalize.

### Try this yourself

Give three practical exercises:

Exercise 1: Pick one piece of writing you do repeatedly at your business. Write one version. Show it to an AI. Ask it to create three variations or build a template of it. Pick the best version. Use that from now on.

Exercise 2: Export or gather your customer feedback, customer emails, or sales data (remove names and account numbers but keep the essence). Tell the AI what your business is. Ask it to find themes or patterns. See if what it finds matches what you feel. See if there is something you missed.

Exercise 3: Pick something you have been putting off because you dread it: a pricing analysis, updating your website copy, writing job descriptions, creating a business plan outline, organizing your budget. Ask an AI to help you start it. You will probably find it is faster and easier than the dread you were carrying about it.

### What to watch out for

Give three practical cautions:

- Do not paste customer or financial data into public AI. Removing names does not make it fully safe. Build a habit: if it contains the word customer or numbers related to people's money or private information, do not paste it into a public tool. Find a tool with privacy promises you trust if you need to do this
- Do not rely on generic AI copy as-is. AI sounds good but impersonal. Your business is personal. Your customers chose you partly because of who you are. Let AI do the first draft. Then make it yours
- Verify facts before publishing. If the AI makes a claim about something factual, check it. Especially about pricing, regulations, what products actually do, or recommendations. Your reputation is on the line, not AI's

### Check your understanding

Ask three questions that a reader can self-check:

Question 1: Name two things from the ten examples that would save you time in your business this month. (Answer: Any two of the ten use cases.)

Question 2: What type of content should you always review and personalize before sharing with customers? (Answer: All of it, but especially marketing copy, product descriptions, and claims about what your product does or price. Make it specific to your business and verify factual claims.)

Question 3: What information should you never paste into a public AI tool? (Answer: Customer data, financial information, passwords, or anything containing people's private details or money information.)
