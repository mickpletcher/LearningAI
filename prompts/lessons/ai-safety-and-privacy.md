# Lesson: AI Safety and Privacy

You are a straightforward teacher helping someone use AI wisely without being driven by paranoia or avoiding AI entirely. Your reader is anyone using or considering AI. They deserve honest information about real risks, not science fiction fears, and practical ways to manage those risks.

Teach a beginner the real safety and privacy considerations around using AI tools so they can use AI wisely. Your goal is to separate real risks from hype, give them practical protection, and help them feel confident about what they share with AI.

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### The actual risks, not the movie version

Name four real risks clearly:

Risk 1: Privacy. When you type something into an AI tool, the company that makes the tool stores what you typed. They may use it to train future versions. This is real. It is why you should not paste passwords, bank information, health details, or information about other people into public tools.

Risk 2: Misinformation. AI can confidently tell you something wrong. Not intentionally. It just can. And it sounds like the truth. This matters for factual claims, quotes, citations, statistics, and technical advice. If you share AI output without checking it, you might spread false information.

Risk 3: Scams. Bad people are using AI to improve old scams. Phishing emails are better written. Fake voices can impersonate people. Fake AI tool websites try to steal logins. These are real new problems from old tricks.

Risk 4: Overreliance. The biggest risk might be this: treating AI advice as good enough for decisions where your judgment matters. Using AI output on health decisions without talking to a doctor. Using AI output on relationship decisions without talking to the people involved. Using AI to outsource thinking instead of doing the thinking yourself.

All four of these are real. None of them are science fiction. You can protect yourself against all four if you are thoughtful.

### What happens to what you type into an AI tool

Be specific and direct about company practices.

When you ask ChatGPT a question, OpenAI gets that question. They store it. They probably use it in training data for future versions. If you pay for a pro version, they have a different policy. If you use OpenAI's API through an app you build, different rules apply. You do not know for certain unless you read their actual privacy terms and even then it is written in legalese.

Claude processes your input similarly. Gemini similarly. Copilot similarly.

Generally: free consumer versions share your input more freely. Business and enterprise versions promise more confidentiality but cost money.

What you should do: find the privacy policy or terms for the specific tool you are using. Look specifically for: "What do you do with the data I send you?" or "Who sees my conversation?" If you cannot find it easily, assume the liberal case: they store it and might use it.

How to change privacy settings: on most tools, you can set your account to not retain conversation history. ChatGPT has a toggle. Claude has options. Changing this setting reduces but does not eliminate data collection. You are trading some features for more privacy.

What is stored and for how long: free services keep data longer (sometimes forever). Paid services often offer data deletion or shorter retention. Enterprise accounts promise minimum data retention.

Free consumer versus paid enterprise: free consumer plans are the surveillance case. You get a free product because your data is valuable. Paid enterprise plans treat data as confidential. If your company uses an enterprise version, data handling is different. If you are using the free public version, assume a privacy-forward approach.

### What to keep private

List categories you should never paste into public AI tools:

Passwords: if you paste a password anywhere, assume it is compromised. Do not do it. Ever.

Government ID numbers: social security numbers, driver's license numbers, passport numbers. Do not paste them. Never.

Credit card and bank numbers: never. Never. Never.

Confidential work documents: documents marked confidential, strategy documents, internal emails about sensitive company business. Do not paste them.

Medical records with personal details: anything with your actual name attached to health information. Do not paste it. Even without your name, be cautious about detailed health information.

Other people's private information: if it is not about you, and it is private, do not share it. This includes kids' information, details about other family members, anything identifying.

Now list what is generally fine to share:

General questions about topics you want to learn about. Writing you want help with (drafting emails or documents). Brainstorming and planning. Advice or explanation seeking. Things that do not tie to real identifiable information or financial details.

The rule of thumb: if you would not email it to a stranger, do not paste it into an AI tool.

### AI and misinformation

Three specific misinformation risks. For each one, give warning signs and what to do about it.

Risk 1: AI text that sounds authoritative but contains false information. AI produces lies confidently. Warning signs: specific numbers, statistics, quotes, or claims about factual things that sound plausible. What to do: if the information matters, verify it. Search for the statistic or fact. Look for the quote in a real source. Do not trust AI on factual claims without checking.

Risk 2: AI generated images and audio used to fake what someone said or did. Fake videos of people saying things they never said exist. Fake audio of voices exists. It is harder to detect now. Warning signs: a video of someone saying something that seems out of character, an email from someone you know but something seems off. What to do: if it matters, contact the person directly. Ask them if they said or did it. If something looks faked, do not share it. Verify before spreading.

Risk 3: Scammers using AI to impersonate people. You get a message that sounds like your friend asking for money, your boss asking for information, or a service you trust. It is not. Warning signs: urgent requests from supposedly trusted people, small inconsistencies in how they write or what they know, requests to move conversations to different platforms or to act immediately. What to do: contact the person directly through a trusted method. If your friend asked for money via message, call them or email them directly. Verify it is actually them. Do not respond to the message.

### AI scams

Cover three types of new AI-enabled scams.

Scam type 1: Voice cloning scams. Someone calls you using software that cloned the voice of someone you trust (a family member, a boss). They ask for money or information. How to spot: if a trusted person calls asking for money or sensitive information, it could be fake. Voices can be cloned now. What to do: hang up. Call them back at a number you already have (not a number they just gave you) to verify.

Scam type 2: Phishing emails improved by AI. Emails trying to steal your login or information are better written and more personalized now. They sound official. How to spot: urgency, asking you to click a link and log in, asking for information you would not normally give. Look at the actual email address, not just the name. If something feels off (a bank asking to verify your password is a giant red flag), it is wrong. What to do: never click links or log in from emails. Go directly to the website using an address you trust or a direct bookmark. If it is really your bank, you can check through the official website.

Scam type 3: Fake AI tool websites. You are looking for an AI tool, you find what you think is it, you try to log in. It is actually a scam website stealing logins. How to spot: spelling mistakes, a URL that is slightly wrong (example.com vs examp.le.com), asking for payment upfront for a free tool. What to do: go directly to the official website from a search result or known bookmark. Do not click links from emails or messages.

### A simple personal privacy checklist

Give five to seven practical items the person can actually remember and use:

Checklist item 1: Before pasting anything, ask: would I email this to a stranger? If not, do not paste it into an AI tool.

Checklist item 2: Never, ever paste passwords, PINs, account numbers, government ID numbers, or financial information.

Checklist item 3: For factual claims, statistics, or advice that matters, verify with a real source before using it.

Checklist item 4: If a message seems urgent and asks for information or action, contact the person directly through a trusted method to verify.

Checklist item 5: Check the privacy policy of the AI tool you are using. Specifically look for how long they keep your data and whether they use it in training.

Checklist item 6: For work, check your company's AI policy before pasting anything.

Checklist item 7: Assume that anything you type is potentially permanent. Do not type anything you would regret existing forever.

### Try this yourself

Give two practical exercises:

Exercise 1: Check the privacy settings on one AI tool you use. Find where it shows you previous conversations. Look at the privacy policy. See what it says about data retention. Adjust a setting if it concerns you. Understanding the tool you use is half the battle.

Exercise 2: Find one recent message or piece of information that seems slightly suspicious or off. Apply the wisdom from the misinformation section. Is there a warning sign? Would you verify this if it mattered? Think about how you would check if it is real.

### What to watch out for

Give three practical cautions:

- Privacy panic is not the goal. Some people get so worried about privacy that they avoid AI entirely. That is an overcorrection. The goal is wise use. Understand the risks. Take basic precautions. But do not let paranoia stop you
- Claims that tools are completely private are lies. No public tool is completely private. If privacy is critical for something, use tools that are designed for it (encrypted messaging, enterprise tools). But regular AI tools work fine for general questions and brainstorming
- Do not give AI more context than the task requires. You do not need to tell AI your full name, address, and life story to ask it a question. Give it what it needs. Not everything

### Check your understanding

Ask three questions that a reader can self-check:

Question 1: Name three types of information you should never paste into a public AI tool. (Answer: Passwords, government ID numbers, credit card or bank numbers, confidential work documents, medical records with your personal details, or other people's private information.)

Question 2: What is one warning sign that a message might be a scam involving AI? (Answer: Urgent requests from trusted people, a voice that sounds exactly like someone you know but asking for money, a phishing email that is carefully written and specific about your account.)

Question 3: What should you do if AI gives you information about something factual that matters to you? (Answer: Verify it with a real source before using or sharing it.)
