# Lesson: Free AI Tools

You are a practical guide helping a beginner find the right free AI tool for what they want to do. Your reader might be retired, working, a parent, a student, or a small business owner. They are new to AI tools and need plain-language guidance without judgment or favoritism.

Teach a beginner what free AI tools exist, what each is primarily good for, and how to match a tool to a task. Your goal is to get them comfortable trying a tool and confident about whether the free version is enough for them.

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### The landscape in plain English

Give a quick orientation. AI tools today fall into two rough categories: general-purpose text-based AI assistants, and specialized tools that do one thing well.

The text assistants are what most beginners should start with. They can write, summarize, explain, brainstorm, and draft just about anything.

Specialized tools handle specific jobs: image generation if you want pictures, transcription if you have audio you want converted to text, grammar checkers if you want to polish writing, document summarizers if you have a big PDF to break down.

Start with a general text assistant if you are new. Add specialized tools when you need them.

### The main free text AI assistants

Write about Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and Copilot in prose paragraphs, not a comparison table. Be honest about differences and never show favoritism.

Claude (made by Anthropic) is known for being thorough and thoughtful. It tends to give you comprehensive answers and will acknowledge when something is uncertain. It works well for writing, analysis, coding help, and thinking through complex problems. Free tier gives you decent access. The main limit is that each conversation resets, so it does not remember your previous chats.

ChatGPT (made by OpenAI) is probably the most familiar to people who have tried AI. It is fast and good at what it does. Free tier works, though it gives you access to an older version of their model. When you want something quick and straightforward, ChatGPT is usually solid. The free version has speed limits during peak hours.

Gemini (made by Google) is good at understanding context and handling nuance. It works well for explaining things, research, and back-and-forth conversation. Free tier is fairly generous. One advantage: Gemini can see web pages if you give it a URL, which the others cannot do as easily. This makes it good for research.

Copilot (made by Microsoft, using OpenAI's technology) works best if you are already in Windows or Edge. It is integrated into those systems, which is convenient. Quality is strong. Free tier is solid, no major limits if you stay within reasonable usage.

No single tool is best for everyone. Different people prefer different ones. Try one and see if it fits what you usually ask AI to do.

### Free tools for specific tasks

Beyond general text assistants, specialized tools exist for specific needs:

Image generation tools like DALL-E (free limited tier), Midjourney (free trial), or Stable Diffusion (free open-source) create pictures from text descriptions. If you need pictures for a project, these work. Free versions have limits on how many pictures you can generate.

Document summarizing: you can almost always do this with general text assistants, but if you use this function constantly, there are tools specialized in it. Upload a PDF or document, get the summary in seconds. Free trials exist.

Audio transcription tools like Whisper (free, open-source), Otter.ai (free tier), or Google Sheets integrated transcription will listen to audio or video and turn it into text. Useful if you record meetings or interviews. Free versions have time limits per month.

Grammar and writing helpers like Grammarly (free tier with basic features) will check your writing and suggest improvements. More focused than a general AI but persistent across all your writing if you use the browser extension.

### How to choose the right tool for your task

Give a simple decision process anyone can actually follow in thirty seconds or less:

First question: Is this a general thinking or writing task? (explaining something, drafting something, brainstorming, analyzing, researching). If yes, use a general text assistant like ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Copilot.

Second question: Do you need to generate, summarize, transcribe, or check something specific? If yes: generating images, use an image tool. Summarizing long documents, use a summary tool or a text assistant. Transcribing audio, use a transcription tool. Checking grammar, use a grammar tool. This is not that hard. Your task tells you what tool to pick.

That is mostly it. For ninety percent of first-time users, the answer is "start with a general text assistant."

### What the free tiers actually give you

Be direct and honest. For a complete beginner who uses AI occasionally, free tiers are enough. Seriously. You will not immediately hit limits.

ChatGPT free tier: you can send maybe 40 messages per three hours during normal times, more at quiet times. That is plenty for someone using AI a couple times a day.

Claude free tier: similar. You get daily message allotments that are generous if you are using it every day.

Gemini free tier: very generous, few practical limits if it is personal use.

Copilot free tier: few limits. Works fine for regular use.

Image generation and transcription tools charge based on what you make. Free versions give you limited credits per month. If you are experimenting, free is enough.

Do not switch to paid until you actually hit a limit and feel frustrated. Most people never do. The free tools are good.

### How to get started in five minutes

Step by step account creation and first message. Make no technical assumptions.

Step 1: Pick one tool. Let us say ChatGPT. Go to openai.com. Click "Sign up" or "Try ChatGPT." Create an account (email or use your Google account).

Step 2: You are now in the chat window. You see a text box at the bottom that says "Send a message." Click in that box.

Step 3: Type a question or request. Something simple: "What are five easy things I could learn how to cook?" or "Help me plan a weekend trip to somewhere I have never been." Do not overthink this.

Step 4: Press Enter or click the Send button. AI will type out an answer. That is it. You have now used AI. Read the answer. If it is helpful, great. If not, try asking a follow-up question or try again.

That is the whole thing. There is no secret. You just ask it something.

### Try this yourself

Give two practical exercises:

Exercise 1: Pick a general text assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Copilot). Create an account (ten seconds, use your Google or Microsoft account to speed it up). Ask it one thing you are curious about or want help with this week. Spend three minutes. See what happens. Most people enjoy the result enough to come back.

Exercise 2: Think of a specialized task: making an image, cleaning up a recording, summarizing a document, or checking writing. Pick the appropriate specialized tool and try the same process: create account, do the task, see what you think.

### What to watch out for

Give three practical cautions:

- Free tier limits are real. When your limit resets, you can use the tool again. These tools are not secretly collecting infinite amounts of free usage. They have limits. That is how they run the free tier. Do not be shocked when you hit a limit
- Privacy: when you paste things into public AI tools, you are sharing it with the company that runs the tool. Do not paste things you would not want them to see. Passwords, financial account details, private health info. That is not AI's problem. That is your choice about what to share with whom
- Tools that market themselves as AI but do not deliver: there are companies that slap "AI-powered" on something that is not really AI or does not actually save time. Ignore the marketing. Try the tool. Does it actually save you time? Does it work for your real task? If not, move on

### Check your understanding

Ask three questions that a reader can self-check:

Question 1: What should a complete beginner start with to use AI for the first time? (Answer: A general text assistant like ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Copilot. Not a specialized tool.)

Question 2: Is the free tier of public AI tools enough for a beginner? (Answer: Yes, free tiers give you plenty to experiment with and learn without paying.)

Question 3: What information should you never paste into a public AI tool? (Answer: Passwords, government IDs, financial account numbers, credit cards, medical records with your personal details, confidential work documents, or other people's private information.)
