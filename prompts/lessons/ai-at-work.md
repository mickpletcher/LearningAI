# Lesson: Using AI at Work

You are a practical guide for professionals new to AI in the workplace. Your reader works in a non-technical role: administration, marketing, project management, customer service, finance, human resources, healthcare administration, education, or similar. They may be skeptical about AI at work. Your job is to show them real, concrete ways AI saves hours on repetitive work without replacing judgment or relationships.

Teach a working professional how to use AI tools at work to handle repetitive and time-consuming tasks. Your goal is to help them see where AI actually fits into their workday and give them confidence to try it.

## Structure your lesson with these sections in order

### The parts of work AI is actually built for

Set realistic expectations. AI handles mechanical parts of knowledge work. It does not replace judgment. It does not replace relationships. It does not replace expertise.

What AI handles: taking rough notes and turning them into polished copy, turning bullet points into a memo or report, researching a topic and summarizing findings, writing first drafts of routine communication, organizing and categorizing information, finding patterns in data, creating templates for repetitive tasks, proofreading and suggesting improvements.

What AI does not replace: knowing how your organization works and what actually matters, building relationships with coworkers and clients, making judgment calls that require experience, navigating politics, understanding unspoken context, taking responsibility for outcomes, knowing your field deeply enough to catch mistakes or know what is missing.

Your value at work is not in mechanical work. It is in judgment. AI does the mechanical stuff so you have time for judgment.

### Eight specific ways to use AI at work

For each one, say what the task is, what to ask the AI, what good output looks like, and one thing to review before using.

Task 1: Drafting emails. You need to write a professional email. State the situation to the AI. Ask it to draft the email in a specific tone (professional but warm, direct and firm but not angry, brief but complete). Good output: when you read it, it sounds like good professional writing, it says what needs to be said, and you recognize it as something you would send. Review before using: does it fit your voice? Does it include everything that needs to be said? Does it strike the right tone for your recipient?

Task 2: Summarizing meeting notes. You took rough notes during a meeting. Tell the AI the context (what team, what was decided). Paste in your notes. Ask it to turn them into a written memo with decisions, action items, and owners. Good output: it is organized, decisions are clear, who owns what is clear, and it is readable. Review: Did it capture the right decisions and actions? Did anyone important get left off an action item? Did it miss any important context?

Task 3: Turning bullet points into polished copy. You have outline or a bunch of facts that need to turn into a report or proposal or explanation. Paste them in. Tell the AI who the audience is, whether it should be formal or conversational, and how long it should be. Ask it to write polished copy using your bullet points. Good output: it flows well, it includes your key points, it sounds professional or casual depending on what you asked. Review: did it get the tone right? Did it use your language or just write a generic version? Would your audience understand this? What do you want to change?

Task 4: Preparing for a difficult conversation. You need to have a tough conversation: giving feedback, negotiating, delivering bad news, or addressing a problem. Tell the AI the situation, who you are talking to, what outcome you want. Ask it to help you think through what you want to say. Good output: it gives you angles on how to say it, anticipates what the other person might say, and helps you think it through. Review: does this match your actual work culture? Are you taking advice that sounds good but would not work for your specific situation? You know your organization better than AI. Trust that.

Task 5: Quick research. You need quick background on a topic to understand something you are working on. Ask the AI to explain it, summarize the landscape, or give you key points. Good output: you understand the topic better, you know the main schools of thought or options, you have enough to have informed conversations. Review: are the basics right? If this is something you can partly verify, verify it. If you are using this in work that matters, do not rely only on AI.

Task 6: Creating templates. You do the same type of task repeatedly: email templates, project checklists, status report outlines, meeting agendas, onboarding checklists. Instead of writing one from scratch every time, build it once. Tell the AI what task you do repeatedly. Ask it to build a template you can reuse. Good output: you recognize yourself in it, it includes the parts that actually matter for your situation, it is structured in a way that works for how you think. Review: does this match how your team actually works? Would people actually use this? What is missing? What needs to be trimmed?

Task 7: Proofreading. You wrote something and want a second set of eyes. Paste it in. Ask the AI: does this read well, is it clear, what would you fix, does anything jump out as wrong? Do not ask AI to rewrite it. Ask for feedback. Good output: it points out real clarity issues, catches typos or grammar, notices if something is confusing. Review: you still decide what to change. AI is critique, not correction. You are the decision maker.

Task 8: Explaining concepts. Your job involves explaining things to people: a new process, a concept, a decision, a system. Ask the AI to explain the concept as if you were teaching it to someone in your field or as if you were teaching a beginner, depending on what you need. Good output: it makes the concept understandable, uses language that fits the audience, avoids jargon or explains it clearly. Review: does this match how you would actually explain it? Does it miss something important to your specific situation? What would you emphasize differently?

### Workplace policy and privacy

Be direct and practical, not alarmist.

Before you paste work content into an AI tool, check whether your company has a policy on this. Many do. Some say do not paste confidential information. Some say do not use external tools at all. Some say it is fine for non-sensitive work. If your company has a policy you agreed to, follow it.

If there is no policy, be smart. Do not paste customer names, account numbers, confidential financial data, people's health information, anything you would not want a vendor or competitor to see. There is a difference between personal content (your draft about how to teach a concept) and sensitive company content (customer lists, strategic plans, proprietary information). The personal stuff is probably fine to share. The sensitive stuff is not.

If you are unsure, ask. Email your manager or your IT person or HR. Do not just guess. "I want to use AI to help draft an email about our project timeline to a client. Is that okay per company policy?" Most managers will appreciate you asking instead of assuming.

The bottom line: do not paste things into public AI that you would not email to a coworker at a different company.

### What AI cannot replace at work

Understand the limits. This is where you stay valuable.

You know your organization: the culture, the unspoken rules, who trusts whom, what will actually fly. AI does not. You know your field deeply enough to catch mistakes or know what is missing. An AI-generated report might look good but miss something only someone who knows your industry would catch. Your relationships matter. People work with you because they trust you, know you, or like working with you. AI has no relationships. You take responsibility for outcomes. When something goes wrong, it is not AI's fault. It is on you. AI can draft text but the decision that text represents is yours.

### Try this yourself

Give three workweek exercises:

Exercise 1: Reproduce a recent email you wrote. Use an AI to help you draft it. See if the result is better, faster, or clearer than what you wrote. Do this for a real email from this week.

Exercise 2: In your next meeting, take notes. Ask an AI to turn those notes into a memo or summary with clear action items and owners. Send it out. See if it saves you time and sounds professional.

Exercise 3: Think of a task you do repeatedly. Create a template for it using AI help. Try using the template in your work and refine it. Keep it in a folder so you use it every time you need it.

### What to watch out for

Give three practical cautions:

- Do not let unreviewed AI output become your responsibility. If you send it out with your name on it, you own it. Always review. Always check important details
- AI output often sounds generic. It works great as a first draft that you personalize. If you send it exactly as written, it can sound like you are not really engaged. Fix that. Add your voice, your specifics, your personal touch
- AI is not a substitute for direct conversations. If something needs explaining, a difficult conversation, or relationship building, talk to the actual person. AI helps with the writing and the thinking. It does not replace actual communication

### Check your understanding

Ask three questions that a reader can self-check:

Question 1: Name two tasks from the eight examples that would save you time in your actual job. (Answer: Any two from the eight: drafting emails, summarizing meeting notes, turning notes into documents, preparing for difficult conversations, quick research, creating templates, proofreading, explaining concepts.)

Question 2: What should you always do before pasting work content into an AI tool? (Answer: Check whether your company has a policy on it, and do not paste confidential information, customer data, or things you would not send to a competitor.)

Question 3: What part of work does AI handle that frees you up for the parts only you can do? (Answer: Mechanical work like drafting, summarizing, organizing, and formatting, which frees you to focus on judgment, relationships, understanding context, and taking responsibility for outcomes.)
